# az-ai v1.8.0 — The Keyboard Becomes a Compiler

*A dispatch from the field, filed late, filed honest.*

---

## I. The 2 AM Problem

Picture it. **Istanbul, 2 AM.** The muezzin has long since gone to bed. You haven't.
You're three cups deep into a French press that has given up on you, and your fingers
hover over the keyboard like a pianist who has forgotten the second movement. You
type the trigger — `;;fix` — and wait.

And wait.

Somewhere, a Docker daemon stirs from its slumber. A Python interpreter is being
born, screaming, into a fresh virtualenv. An OpenAI SDK is being imported, module
by module, dependency by dependency, like a bureaucrat stamping forms in triplicate.
By the time the suggestion arrives, the thought that prompted it has already packed
its bags and left for Vienna.

This, friends, is the text-expander's cruelest joke: the faster you *think*, the
slower the machine feels. Espanso fires in single-digit milliseconds. AutoHotkey
reads your mind before you've finished having the thought. And then — right at the
moment of inspiration — you hand the baton to a runtime that takes **400
milliseconds** just to say *hello*.

We decided, somewhere between the third cup and the fourth, that this was
unacceptable.

## II. What We Built, and What It Costs to Start It

**az-ai v1.8.0** is a command-line companion to Azure OpenAI. But describing it that
way is like calling the Orient Express "a train." Technically correct. Spiritually
a crime.

What it actually is: a **single-file, 9-megabyte native binary** that cold-starts
in **5.4 milliseconds**. No JIT warm-up. No framework tax. No apology. You type
`;;fix`, Espanso fires, the binary runs, the model responds, the text lands in
your editor before you've finished exhaling.

It is — and I mean this without hyperbole, because the benchmarks are in the
repository and Puddy has run them — **approximately 10× faster than a ReadyToRun
build, and approximately 75× faster than the Dockerized equivalent** for this
workload. The keyboard, at last, becomes a compiler.

## IV. The Numbers

The claim that matters, measured on the same hardware, same workload (a one-shot
text-injection trigger), cold start to first byte:

| Runtime                       | Cold start | Relative |
| ----------------------------- | ---------: | -------: |
| **Native AOT (this release)** |   **5.4 ms** | **1.0×** |
| ReadyToRun (R2R)              |    ~54 ms  |    ~10×  |
| Docker container              |   ~405 ms  |    ~75×  |

Five-point-four milliseconds. Faster than a camera shutter. Faster than the
refresh on a 144 Hz display. Faster than the synaptic delay between deciding
to press a key and actually pressing it.

At these speeds, the AI stops being a tool you *invoke* and starts being a
reflex. Espanso fires. The binary answers. You keep typing. The whole exchange
is over before your peripheral vision has time to notice it happened.

## V. Try It (Three Commands)

```bash
git clone https://github.com/SchwartzKamel/azure-openai-cli.git
cd azure-openai-cli && make install
az-ai --help
```

That's the whole ceremony. No virtualenv. No `pip`. No Docker Desktop prompting
you, passive-aggressively, for an update. A `make install` and a binary. The way
God and Kernighan intended.

## VI. An Anecdote, Because Peterman Insists

*It was the rainy season in Chiang Mai. I was working from a guesthouse above
a noodle shop, the fan spinning lazily, the smell of lemongrass rising through
the floorboards. My laptop battery was at seven percent and the power had been
out for an hour. I had a deadline. I had a client. I had, crucially, an idea —
one of those rare ones that arrive fully formed, like a telegram.*

*I typed the trigger. Five milliseconds later, the completion was on my screen.
I typed again. Five milliseconds. Again. Five. The binary sipped electrons
the way a monk sips tea — deliberately, sparingly, with gratitude. By the time
the lights came back on I had shipped the fix, closed the laptop, and ordered a
second bowl of khao soi.*

*This is what a 9-megabyte binary buys you. Not speed, exactly. Something
rarer. It buys you the luxury of forgetting the tool exists.*

## VII. What's Next — v1.9 and the Streaming Agent

We are working on a **streaming agent mode** for v1.9: long-running,
interactive, tool-using conversations that stream tokens the moment the model
produces them. Same cold-start budget. Same 9 MB footprint. More rope, more
capability, same philosophy — *get out of the user's way*.

Expect a preview on `main` within weeks. Expect the GA when the tests — all
of them, every last one — are green.
